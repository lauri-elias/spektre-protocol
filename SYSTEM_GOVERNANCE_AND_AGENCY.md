## Purpose

This document defines **system governance**:  
how decision-rights are distributed and exercised when an individual operates inside institutional, medical, or legal environments.

The goal is **coherent governance**, not total control.

---

## Core Principle

**Governance = management of decision-rights**

Decisions in complex environments are shared between:
- the individual (human agency)
- institutional actors (healthcare, legal systems)
- technical parameters (medication, diagnostics, constraints)

Clarity of roles prevents conflict, anxiety, and responsibility diffusion.

---

## Authority Layers

### 1. Human Agency (Primary Decision Owner)

The individual remains the final owner of decisions related to their life, actions, and values.

- Responsibility cannot be transferred.
- Advice does not equal command.
- Consent remains central.

**Invariant:** 1 = 1  
Actions originate from a single accountable agent.

---

### 2. Institutional Nodes (Advisory and Gatekeeping Role)

Healthcare professionals and institutions function as **expert evaluators**.

- Role: provide assessments, data, and recommendations
- Authority: manage access to resources and permissions
- Limitation: they do not replace personal agency

Effective governance favors **cooperation**, not opposition.

---

### 3. Technical Parameters (Medication and Tools)

Medication and other technical interventions are treated as **system parameters**, not identity modifiers.

- Purpose: stabilize biological or cognitive substrate
- Evaluation: based on observed effect, not belief
- Use: conditional, adjustable, reversible when appropriate

Tools support agency; they do not override it.

---

## Exception States (Involuntary or High-Control Situations)

Involuntary care or high-restriction environments can be modeled as **exception states**.

- Comparable to safe-mode operation in technical systems
- Triggered by perceived instability or risk
- Temporarily shifts authority toward institutions

Exit from an exception state occurs through demonstrated stability and trust restoration.

This framing reduces confusion and supports recovery of autonomy.

---

## Trust Model

Trust is operational, not emotional.

- Self-trust: confidence in oneâ€™s ability to regulate behavior and decisions
- Institutional trust: willingness to receive input without surrendering agency
- Mutual trust enables authority to normalize

Clear roles reduce defensive behavior on all sides.

---

## Practical Guidelines

1. Treat professional input as data, not identity definition  
2. Evaluate interventions based on functional outcomes  
3. Maintain routines that support baseline stability  
4. Communicate clearly and minimally in institutional contexts  
5. Avoid unnecessary engagement with destabilizing narratives  

Governance improves when noise is reduced.

- The environment constrains
- The individual commits

Agency is preserved through clarity, cooperation, and responsibility ownership.
