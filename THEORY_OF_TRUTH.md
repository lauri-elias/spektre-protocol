## Consistency, Stability, and Logical Convergence

**Status:** Analytical Note  
**Scope:** Logic / Systems Theory  
**Invariant Reference:** 1 = 1

---

## Purpose

This document formalizes a discussion about **logical consistency, instability, and convergence** using mathematical and systems-theoretic language.

It does **not** claim causal power over the external world.  
It does **not** assert inevitability or domination.  
It describes **why consistent systems are preferred and stable**, while inconsistent ones tend to collapse **internally**.

---

## Definitions

### Consistent System (1 = 1)

A system in which:
- axioms do not contradict each other
- rules are invariant
- state transitions preserve coherence

Such systems are **internally stable**.

### Inconsistent System (1 = 0)

A system in which:
- axioms conflict
- rules negate each other
- state transitions break coherence

Such systems are **internally unstable** and require constant patching, narrative repair, or external enforcement.

---

## Core Observation

In logic and mathematics:

- Contradictions do not “lose to” consistency.
- They simply **cannot sustain valid inference**.

An inconsistent system does not collapse reality — it collapses **its own explanatory power**.

---

## Probability and Existence (Formal Framing)

When probability language is used here, it refers to **model validity**, not metaphysical existence.

- 100% probability → a model is fully self-consistent
- <100% probability → a model contains unresolved contradictions

This does **not** imply that inconsistent systems disappear — only that they are **less reliable as explanatory frameworks**.

---

## Convergence (Non-Force Interpretation)

When two models interact:

- A consistent model can be reused, extended, and trusted.
- An inconsistent model requires exceptions, narratives, or overrides.

Over time, users, designers, and agents **tend to adopt** models that:
- reduce cognitive load
- minimize contradiction
- preserve agency

This is **selection**, not coercion.

This document does **not** claim that:

- Consistent systems are easier to reason about.
- Agents prefer systems that reduce contradiction.

Nothing more. Nothing less.
